Thick vs. Thin Copyright

The scope of copyright protection

THICK COPYRIGHT

Creative works

  • Novels, poetry, music
  • Original artwork, photos
  • Creative software interfaces
  • Architectural designs

THIN COPYRIGHT

Functional works

  • Phone directories, databases
  • Software algorithms
  • Basic user interfaces
  • Factual compilations

BROAD PROTECTION

Copying, adaptation, distribution, performance

NARROW PROTECTION

Literal copying only, not independent creation

Key Principle

The more creative and original a work, the "thicker" its copyright protection

Speaker Notes

  • Thick copyright applies to highly creative works with substantial originality.
  • Thin copyright applies to functional works, facts, and minimal creative elements.
  • This distinction is crucial for understanding what aspects of software are protectable.
  • Algorithms and functional aspects of software typically receive thin or no protection.

Non-Human Authorship

The human authorship requirement

The Monkey Selfie Case

Naruto v. Slater (2018)

A crested macaque named Naruto took a selfie using photographer David Slater's camera. The monkey's "selfie" went viral, and PETA sued on behalf of Naruto, claiming the monkey owned the copyright.

Court Decision: The Ninth Circuit held that animals cannot own copyrights because copyright law requires human authorship.

Key Principle

"Copyright law requires human authorship"

This principle applies to AI-generated works as well

Speaker Notes

  • The monkey selfie case established that non-human entities cannot own copyrights.
  • This principle has been extended to AI-generated works in cases like Thaler v. Perlmutter.
  • Human authorship remains a fundamental requirement for copyright protection.

Ross Headnotes Case

Originality requirement in practice

Matthew Bender & Co. v. West Publishing Co.

Background: West Publishing claimed copyright in "headnotes" - brief summaries of legal cases that appear at the beginning of case reports.

The Issue

Are headnotes copyrightable, or are they merely factual summaries lacking originality?

Court Decision

The Second Circuit held that West's headnotes were copyrightable because they involved:

  • Creative selection of key legal points
  • Original expression in summarizing complex legal concepts
  • Sufficient creativity to meet the originality requirement

Significance: This case demonstrates that even factual summaries can be copyrightable if they involve sufficient creative choices and original expression.

Speaker Notes

  • Headnotes are brief summaries of legal cases that appear at the beginning of case reports.
  • The case shows that factual content can be copyrightable if it involves creative selection and arrangement.
  • This is relevant to AI training data - even factual content may have copyright protection.

AI Authorship Cases

Recent developments in AI and copyright

Thaler v. Perlmutter (2023)

Facts: Stephen Thaler sought copyright for "A Recent Entrance to Paradise," created by his AI system "Creativity Machine"

Outcome: U.S. Copyright Office denied registration

Reasoning: No human authorship required

Li v. Liu (China, 2023)

Facts: Human used AI to generate image with significant input and refinement

Outcome: Beijing Internet Court granted copyright

Reasoning: Human creative input sufficient

Key Takeaway

The level of human involvement in AI-generated works determines copyright eligibility. Mere prompting may not be sufficient, but significant creative input and refinement can establish human authorship.

Speaker Notes

  • Thaler v. Perlmutter established that purely AI-generated works lack human authorship.
  • Li v. Liu shows that significant human input can establish authorship in AI-assisted works.
  • These cases highlight the importance of human creative involvement for copyright protection.

Works Made for Hire

17 U.S.C. ¬ß101 definition

A "work made for hire" is:

1. Employee Works

Work created by an employee within the scope of employment

Example: Software code written by a programmer employed by a tech company

2. Commissioned Works

Specially ordered or commissioned work falling under specific categories with written agreement

Categories: Contributions to collective works, parts of motion pictures, translations, compilations, instructional texts, tests, atlases

Key Point

In works made for hire, the employer or commissioning party is considered the legal author and owns the copyright, not the individual creator.

Speaker Notes

  • Works made for hire are a major exception to the general rule that the creator owns the copyright.
  • This is particularly relevant in employment contexts and certain types of commissioned works.
  • Clear contractual agreements are essential to determine ownership in commissioned works.

Read the Full Statute
